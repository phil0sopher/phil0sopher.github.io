YUVUE is an online service platform, which provides local news broadcasters and brands to have access to the user-generated content, as well as tools to find, sort, and display content from social media.

Our Backstory

William and his team came to us with a bare bones product, looking to design the public-facing side of YuVue Community™, which will allow local news broadcasters to aggregate and manage user-generated photo and video content via web and/or app.

They asked us to generate several deliverables:

  1. An app “for specialized display and upload of photos and videos,” which could be YuVue branded or white-labelled.
  2. A “general purpose” app for broadcasters that would also include news, weather, traffic, news, etc.
  3. Multiple responsive layout templates for microsites that could be white-labelled.

Getting Started

Our team immediately discussed our method and focus, in order to create a quality product for our client in only 2 ½ weeks. We conducted domain research as well as a general competitive analysis of UGC platforms as well as crowdsourced news platforms.

However, I struggled to understand how all of their deliverables would be able to fit with each other, given the time we had.

We addressed our questions and immediately set up a meeting with our client. During our meeting we decided to narrow our scope, so we could create a quality product. In the end, we focused on creating a local sports app, when we found out our client recently developed a partnership with Scorestream(a crowdsourced sports app).

Research

With our new plan, set in motion, we immediately scheduled Skype and telephone interviews with sports fan and sports reporting-related SMEs in addition to our initial news reporting-related interviews.

The Interviews

We interviewed 4 Subject Matter Experts and 5 general users. From this, we used affinity diagramming to find key points from our interviews and found several insights.

Interview Takeaways
SMEs
  •    Broadcasters have existing relationships/contracts with 3rd party content sharing services: “For the most part, we use our own footage or footage from our affiliates. It would be impossible to cover national stories without them.”
  •    Complicated restrictions and rules for content used from social media: “You have to be very careful in the industry, when it comes to using video that is shaorange by other people.”
  •    Social media is generally used as a platform for engaging with followers, not so much for getting content: “I don’t expect a response… it’s more about putting something funny or witty out there.”
  •    Fans have difficulty knowing what to say to celebrities: “We will take photos from fans, but post it onto social media”
Users
  •    Twitter is the go-to source for live, convenient sports updates and user commentary: “Reading a live feed on Twitter is like having a broadcaster at the game”
  •    Facebook provides a convenient community for high school sports teams and their parents to share photos and get game updates
  •    Social media is generally used as a platform for engaging with followers, not so much for getting content: “I don’t expect a response… it’s more about putting something funny or witty out there.”
  •     People want play-by-plays: “It’s hard to find a play-by-play on ESPN’s website.”

Define

Once we had these insights, we used the material as well as focused on our primary users to define our problem.

Busy Soccer Mom
  • • When she can’t make it to the game, depends on updates from parents through texting and calling
  • • Relies on Facebook for information and sharing of pictures every game
  • • Her children and their friends have grown up together; she wants updates for the whole team

Sports Fanatic
  • • Needs an easy way to track scores and highlights of multiple teams
  • • Follows his old high school team and tracks their record
  • • Keeps tabs on scores from the local newspaper’s sports blog

Our Problem

Our main personas made us realize we had another main problem:

" Sports fans need a centralized platform where they can create digital communities around local amateur sports that will enable them to:

  • • Easily share and view details about local games with each other
  • • Connect with the team and fellow fans (their local community)
  • • Feel like they are at the game when they can’t be "

To address this problem, we created design principles based on our research as a design framework.

Design Principles
  •    Be part of the local community: Enable users to join and develop an active community of amateur local sport players, parents and fans.
  •    Feel like you’re at the game, even when you aren’t: Create an immersive “front row” experience that combines comments, updates, photos and videos.
  •    All your local sports content in one place: Everything should be available to users in just a few steps, with the best of Twitter, Facebook, Score Stream, and even the local paper into one convenient app.
  •    Leverage and embrace existing social networks: People aren’t going to quit Twitter or Facebook, so consider integrating with existing social networks and relying on their mental models as much as possible.

Ideate & Learn

We continued our ideation process by discussing the general user flow of following a team and posting content to a game. In the end, we chose 3 design directions:

  • • A Snapchat story type of app that would allow fans to contribute to a team or game ‘story’
  • • A visual, content-focused collage of teams you follow
  • • A gamified feed that would encourage participation

Testing

We tested the concepts with 5 users. During this time, we took notes of their thought process and had them give our concepts net promoter scores when they were finished.

The collage concept was rated the highest with a 8.67/10 compaorange to the other two, which were both rated 7.33/10. Feedback was good for this concept; however we identified and addressed several problems that first needed to be addressed.

Our Solutions
  •  Clarify the add buttons throughout all prototypes
  •  Consider more fun ways to play with the content (incorporate filters, gamification?)
  •  Find a way to better organize the feed between previous and current games

Refining

We moved forward with our wireframes & prototype, now that we had our validated concept. We decided on our picks of creating wireframes for an iOS app, Android app, and a responsive microsite version of the concept. The project was deliveorange in time and the client showcased our wireframes and prototype to several news outlets 2 days afterwards.

Insights

Further Considerations:
  • Allocating more resources to the microsite wireframes

    Since our time to create our wireframes was over two days, we did not prioritize coming up with several use cases for the website. As a result, the website wireframe closely resembled the mobile versions. I would conduct research to understand the use cases for the microsite.

  • Exploring additional features

    We created a simple app that focused solely on games. I would like to have people put in content for other events, such as team hangouts and practices. Also, I would like to find some way to incorporate player and team statistics because there would be many use cases for those.

What I Learned
  • Understanding and being “Agile”

    We had to pivot and narrow down our direction during this project. I’m glad for the experience because it taught me to communicate and check in with my team as well as the client; this was to ensure we were on the same page and picked the right designs.

  • Prioritizing user experience

    The client wanted us to create wireframes in the time that we had; we could have generated all of their deliverables but they would not succeed in the long run. I believe prioritizing the user experience over business desires was beneficial to the client. Because user experience is based on research that looks into the audience’s desires and needs; the audience would come back,when they perceive their interaction with the product as a good experience.
